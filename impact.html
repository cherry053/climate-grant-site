One story. Multiplied nationally.

Imagine what
she does next.

That 17-year-old in rural Queensland — the one watching her family's property dry up for the third season. She applies. One page. Her local teacher verifies. She gets $2,000.

She builds a rainwater capture system for her school. It saves 180,000 litres in the first year. Three neighbouring schools replicate the design. She earns enough reward points to attend a national youth climate conference in Brisbane. She meets a water engineer who becomes her mentor. She applies for a $15,000 project grant the following year.

None of that happens without the first $2,000. None of that happens without a system that was built for her.

"I had the idea for three years before anyone gave me a way to start." — composite voice, rural youth survey

What transforms

Three things
that become possible.

🔬

Local innovation

Rural youth design solutions grounded in their communities — water systems, renewable microgrids, First Nations land management practices. Solutions that top-down national programs never reach because they don't know the land the way these young people do.

🙋

Equal participation

When young Australians from every postcode see themselves represented in climate leadership, broader civic engagement rises with it. The reward system makes contribution publicly visible — and builds a generation who know their action counts.

🌱

Measurable climate outcomes

Thousands of small, locally-grounded wins accumulate into national progress. Distributed community-level action achieves outcomes centralised programs cannot — and generates data that feeds directly into government climate reporting.
