The Problem

They see it.
They feel it.

Rural youth live the consequences of climate change every day.
They understand it better than most.

Scroll
Lived Experience

But they can't be
part of the solution.

A 17-year-old in rural Queensland watches her family's cattle property go dry for the third season running. She has ideas — a community water recycling initiative, a local renewable energy co-op. She has read about it. She has thought about it for years.

But the nearest grant workshop is four hours away. The application requires an institutional sponsor. Her school doesn't have a climate program. Her town doesn't have a climate network. She is ready. The system is not built for her.

"I know what needs to happen. I just don't know how to make anyone listen."
— composite voice, rural youth survey

No funding. No transport. No access.
Barrier 01 — Distance

300km.

The average distance a rural youth must travel
to attend a climate grant information session or workshop.

Barrier 02 — Gatekeeping

The application requires
what rural youth don't have.

Most Australian youth climate grants require institutional backing — a university, an NGO, or a registered community organisation as sponsor. For urban youth with existing networks, this is easy. For rural youth, it is often impossible.

Without that sponsor, strong ideas are rejected before a single word of the proposal is read.
